Pollution Solutions

How Trash Effects The Land

“Soil is the earth’s fragile skin that anchors all life on Earth. It is comprised of countless species that create a dynamic and complex ecosystem and is among the most precious resources to humans.” (WWF) Trash can release toxic chemicals into the soil. The economic balance of an ecosystem can also be affected by the chemical contamination of soil, making it hard for plants to grow there. Plants are unable to adapt to the rapid change in soil composition, causing them to die in large numbers. As an additional problem, fungi and bacteria that bind the soil decrease, making the land even more uninhabitable for plants as soil erosion occurs. All ecosystems depend on primary producers as the start of a food chain, so healthy and fertile soil is incredibly important. Also, another harmful environmental occurrence to soil is acid rain. Acid rain can be caused by nitrogen oxides, which are released into the air by burning trash. Acid rain can weaken trees by stripping away the protective film on leaves and stunting growth. Acid rain also harms bodies of water by raising the acidity of the water, killing fish.

How Trash Effects the Water

In the United States, 40% of rivers and 46% of lakes are too polluted for fishing, swimming, and aquatic life. They are polluted with marine debris: trash released into a lake, sea, ocean, or waterway. 5.25 trillion pieces of plastic reside in our oceans, which is 13 times more than 400 billion stars in the Milky Way. Altogether, they amount to 100 million tons of plastic in Earth’s oceans. Another 60 billion pounds is projected to be produced this year, which will most likely enter the seas. It will become more costly to treat water and affected marine life as more garbage and chemicals flow into the oceans. Debris in the water, especially plastic, endangers marine creatures. For example, sea turtles that accidentally consume plastic die slowly as toxic substances break down inside and fill them with gas. This causes the animals to float to the surface where they die of starvation or become easy prey.

Rubbish destroys aquatic ecosystems and requires more money to restore and preserve them. Even then, the damaged environments may never fully recover and their degradation may be too late to reverse. Reefs across the world fight harmful microorganisms that tear the coral’s skin so pathogens can infect them. Swaths of reefs die due to being susceptible to the bacteria from plastics, weakened by warm waters, a condition known as bleaching. The problem of trash and plastic is especially damaging to coral reefs as they are marine biodiversity hotspots and can almost be permanently destroyed.

How Trash Effects the Air

Air pollution is caused by vehicle emissions, fossil fuels, industrial activity, and the burning of trash. It contributes to heart disease, stroke, and lung cancer- all of which were top contributors to death in 2016. Outdoor working-class women and men exposed to the pollutants, such as construction workers and crossing guards, have a higher chance of acquiring and dying from diseases. As a result, their countries’ economies suffer. Even indoors, no one is safe from air pollution. It can be caused by a seemingly harmless task in developing nations: cooking. In 2016, an astonishing 3.8 million people died from indoor pollution, mainly women and children. Indoor pollution is caused by fuels such as kerosene and wood, which are burned during tasks such as cooking or lighting lamps. It leads to pneumonia, which was the leading cause of death for children under 5. However, over 630 million children are exposed to air pollution, and an overwhelming majority worldwide are exposed to higher levels of PM 2.5 pollution than recommended by the World Health Organization.

Our Solution

Some countries are burning their waste with incinerators or simply on the streets. Trash goes to landfills and gets buried under the ground. Other times, trash gets dumped into the oceans through rivers. Countries who are exported trash have the necessary machinery but even then, the pollutants disposing of trash produces is still harmful to the environment.

Raise public awareness on the dangers of trash, create websites with tips on how to limit household waste. One way is to use reusable bags, not plastic and use water bottles, not the plastic ones. Countries can develop AI and robots to clean up the environment and pool money to fund developing nations with waste management.

Robots collect food from houses to turn into compost. People deposit excess food in a robot stationed on streets. The robot is collected and the food is turned into compost. The compost is deposited in farmland as nutrients for crops.

Network on Land: Plastic, metal, and paper robots programmed to recognize and collect specific garbage. These moving robots replace sidewalk trash cans. Once full, they send out a signal for retrieval and empty their contents at facilities.

Network in Sea: Robots equipped with scanners, programmed to scan seafloors for anything that doesn’t naturally belong in the environment. They collect the trash and debris, then return to surface to empty contents.
